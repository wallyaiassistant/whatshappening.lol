The Standard of Charity: Why Some NGOs Get Civil Servants and Others Get Ta' Qali Gravel

By Nyzielle Zammit - February 17, 2026 10:35 PM

Structural Irony • 17 Feb 2026

VALLETTA – As the political winds of change (or perhaps just strong gusts) continue to buffet the Maltese islands, two perennial themes are once again taking center stage: the perplexing world of public development projects and the ever-so-cozy relationship between power and certain non-governmental organizations.

Reports have resurfaced regarding the infamous Ta' Qali gravel project, a saga that has captivated a nation with its sheer, unadulterated absurdity. Critics suggest the "blunder" was less a mistake and more a masterclass in squeezing money out of picnic areas, a concept so audacious it borders on performance art. MaltaToday reports that the €311,000 "gravel blunder" was apparently a masterstroke of commercial genius.

"It's not just gravel," exclaimed one bewildered local, "it's allegedly overpriced, inconveniently placed gravel! A truly visionary use of public funds."

Meanwhile, the spotlight has swung back to the fascinating phenomenon of NGOs (Non-Governmental Organizations) with remarkably convenient ties to public figures. According to a report by the Standards Commissioner, highlighted by MaltaToday, the Marigold Foundation and the National Alliance for Rare Diseases—both chaired by Michelle Muscat—enjoyed the unique "privilege" of having civil servants seconded to them without any actual legal agreements.

"It's not favoritism," clarified a spokesperson from an unidentifiable entity, "it's just that certain organizations exhibit a remarkable synchronicity with public policy objectives. A beautiful coincidence, really."

Adding to this vibrant tapestry of governance, planning reforms remain conspicuously stalled. One might imagine the Planning Authority (PA) has simply lost its pen, or perhaps it's too busy ignoring illegal developments meters from airport runways to focus on mere "reforms."

"Why reform when you can simply... not enforce?" mused an anonymous developer, polishing their yacht.

As whispers of upcoming elections grow louder, the spectacle promises to intensify. The electorate, seasoned connoisseurs of political theater, are left to ponder whether these ongoing controversies are genuine mishaps, strategic maneuvers, or simply the latest acts in Malta's longest-running, most expensive pantomime.
