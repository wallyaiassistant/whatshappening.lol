Government Masters Ancient Art of Strategic Deafness Over Halland Hotel Fiasco

By Shunique Farrugia Â· 21/02/26, 1:08 PM

In what appears to be a case of selective hearing, Malta's government seems to have developed a curious inability to address direct questions about the Halland Hotel situation whenever accountability is mentioned in the same conversation.

While Times of Malta has been persistently covering this story with detailed reporting, other outlets have shown varying levels of interest. ONE News has remained relatively quiet on the matter, perhaps waiting for clearer official statements before committing to coverage.

Meanwhile, MaltaToday managed some coverage though not prominently featured, while NET News gave it somewhat more attention. The coverage disparity across different outlets suggests this story may merit more widespread reporting attention.

The government's response strategy appears to follow a familiar pattern of measured communication, though critics might argue it resembles strategic avoidance. Ministers have maintained professional composure while seeming to develop remarkably selective attention spans when certain topics arise.

"Questions remain about the timeline and commitments made," observes one political commentator. The administration's communication approach, while diplomatically consistent, leaves some transparency advocates wanting clearer answers.

Sources suggest that Cabinet discussions now include careful consideration of media messaging, which is standard practice but might benefit from more proactive engagement with difficult topics rather than reactive damage control.

The Opposition has naturally seized on this communication gap, though their own track record on transparency during their time in government wasn't without similar challenges. Political memory in Malta tends to be conveniently selective across party lines.

As this situation continues to develop, one hopes that constructive dialogue might eventually replace what currently resembles a sophisticated version of covering one's ears and humming loudly until uncomfortable questions go away.

The whole affair demonstrates that in Maltese politics, the art of strategic non-response has been refined to an almost theatrical level, regardless of which party happens to be in power at the time.
