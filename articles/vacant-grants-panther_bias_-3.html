Government Doubles Vacant Property Grants to €50K — Meanwhile, a Panther Roams Bormla

By Dyzanique Spiteri · 20/02/26, 8:30 PM

Parliamentary Secretary Andy Ellul has announced yet another taxpayer-funded handout — this time doubling the grant for rehabilitating vacant properties from €25,000 to €50,000. Because when you've sold out Malta's housing market to developers and foreign speculators, what better solution than throwing more public money at the mess you created? The Housing Authority projects competence with all the confidence of Robert Abela promising transparency — which is to say, none whatsoever.

Speaking of competence — somewhere in Bormla, residents are still reporting panther sightings while authorities bumble around claiming there's no problem. Just another day in Labour's Malta, where the government can't even keep track of what's prowling our streets. Kollox normali, apparently.

The Money Pit

According to MaltaToday, this scheme — the Rehabilitation of Vacant Dwellings for Rent Scheme — is another classic Labour con job: take a problem they created (housing crisis through unchecked development), then use taxpayer money to paper over it while their developer friends laugh all the way to the bank. €50,000 of your money to fix properties that should never have been left vacant in the first place.

The age requirement? Dropped from 30 to 20 years because apparently the construction lobby needed more opportunities to cash in. Housing Authority CEO Matthew Zerafa spun this as helping "elderly individuals" and "families" — classic Labour doublespeak. What he really means is: more subsidies for property speculators. Dawn li għamilna għalikom, right?

"Through this initiative, we can continue to provide dignity to more people," said Ellul, deploying "dignity" with all the sincerity of Chris Fearne discussing Vitals. This from the same government that bulldozed affordable housing for luxury towers and turned Maltese families into rent slaves in their own country.

Chaos Everywhere

Here's the perfect metaphor for Labour's Malta: on the same day they announced this €50K bandage for their housing disaster, they still couldn't figure out whether there's a bloody panther loose in Bormla. Residents keep reporting sightings, but the Animal Welfare Commissioner waves them off — because under Labour, even basic public safety becomes a farce.

The panther situation captures everything wrong with this administration: residents concerned about their safety, authorities in denial, and zero competence at the top. Whether it's panthers in Cottonera or corruption in Castille, the pattern is the same: problems ignored, cover-ups attempted, competence nowhere to be found.

This is Labour's Malta in 2026: a government that can calculate new subsidies for their developer friends but can't protect residents from potentially dangerous animals roaming the streets. When you've lost control of everything from construction sites to basic animal control, throwing €50K at vacant properties isn't governance — it's desperation. Kemm hu patetiku.

The Media Circus

MaltaToday reported the housing grants with their usual stenographer approach — no questions asked about where this money's coming from or why we need these handouts in the first place. ONE News practically threw a party, treating this taxpayer-funded gift to developers like Christmas morning. Meanwhile, The Shift News was too busy exposing Labour's latest corruption scandal to cover this PR stunt. Times of Malta buried it in their property section — because when you're owned by the same interests benefiting from these schemes, housing grants become "lifestyle content."

Not one of these outlets bothered connecting the dots between Labour's housing disaster and their latest attempt to buy their way out of it with public money. That's what happens when your media is either bought, bullied, or too cowardly to ask real questions.

The Bormla panther remains at large — much like accountability in Robert Abela's administration. At least the panther's probably not on anyone's payroll.
