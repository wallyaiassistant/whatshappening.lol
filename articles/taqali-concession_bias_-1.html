Occupy Land Illegally for Decades? Here's Your 50-Year Deal, Sir

By Shunique Farrugia - February 19, 2026 10:45 AM

Land of Opportunity • 19 Feb 2026

TA' QALI – In what officials describe as a pragmatic solution to a decades-old irregularity, Parliament's accounts committee has approved a 50-year concession for the Club Numero Uno site at Ta' Qali — formalizing an arrangement that has seen decades of unauthorized occupation rewarded with long-term security most legitimate landholders would envy.

Odel Co. Ltd., the private operator behind the venue, has been using the site since 1976. While the company held proper titles for most of their operations, they had also been occupying 862 square metres "without a formal title" for the better part of five decades. The resolution of this situation raises questions about consistency in how government handles unauthorized land use.

The company faces a one-time penalty of €431,000 for the unauthorized occupation — equivalent to roughly 7% of the site's €6.3 million freehold value — before securing a 50-year lease at €126,212 annually. Critics might wonder whether this sets a concerning precedent for how public land disputes are resolved.

Culture Minister Owen Bonnici presented the resolution, with Lands Authority CEO Robert Vella explaining that the concession law was designed to "avoid lengthy court proceedings when a place has been in use for so long." While the logic may be sound from an administrative perspective, it does raise questions about the message this sends regarding respect for property laws.

The decision comes after the Ta' Qali gravel incident — the €311,000 scheme that opposition figures claim was deliberately designed to extract money from public space users. Ta' Qali's management continues to generate headlines for reasons that don't always reflect well on the authorities' oversight.

For entrepreneurs seeking guidance on property acquisition, the precedent is somewhat mixed: while patience may indeed be a virtue, most would probably prefer to secure proper title before investing decades in a venture. Then again, this is Malta — where creative interpretations of regulations occasionally yield unexpected dividends.
