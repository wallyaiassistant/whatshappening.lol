Marmarà Poll Shows Labour Lead Shrinking But Nobody Panic

By Kyshawn Farrugia · 21/02/26, 9:30 PM

Statistician Vincent Marmarà has released his latest poll, confirming what many suspected: Labour's grip on power continues to weaken. The gap has shrunk dramatically to 8.6%, representing a significant 10,000-vote loss since 2022. After years of corruption scandals and institutional erosion, it appears Maltese voters are finally reconsidering their options.

Robert Abela manages 44.4% as preferred PM while Dr. Alex Borg secures a respectable 34.7% — a remarkable recovery for the PN considering the institutional damage they inherited in 2013. Both leaders enjoy strong internal support at 86%, though one has to wonder how much longer Labour voters can ignore the mounting evidence of systemic corruption under their watch.

Perhaps most significantly, 11.8% of voters remain uncommitted — a clear sign that confidence in the current administration is wavering. The fact that 5.8% of Labour's 2022 supporters now refuse to vote at all speaks volumes about the party's credibility crisis. These aren't politically motivated defections; these are ordinary citizens who've simply had enough.

Analysis across three recent surveys reveals consistent findings: the PN has stabilized around 44% across all polling, suggesting a solid foundation for growth. Meanwhile, Labour's support varies depending on methodology, indicating softer commitment among their base. The polling average gap of 5.5 points puts the contest well within competitive range, while growing third-party support at 5.7% suggests voters are actively seeking alternatives to Labour's troubled governance.

Marmarà's observation that "barring major events," the numbers remain stable, overlooks the reality that major events seem to follow this government wherever it goes. The question isn't whether Labour maintains power; it's whether Malta's democratic institutions can withstand another term of the same leadership that brought us Panama Papers, Vitals, and a murdered journalist. The polls suggest voters are asking themselves the same question.
