3,487 New Apartments Approved in One Quarter: Malta Builds Faster Than It Can Spell "Sustainability"

By Zykelle Galea - February 19, 2026 9:00 PM

Concrete Jungle • 19 Feb 2026

VALLETTA – The National Statistics Office announced today that 3,487 new dwellings were approved in the last quarter of 2025 — a staggering 54% increase that raises serious questions about Malta's development strategy. Three-quarters are apartments, with the average building permit now spawning 5.8 dwellings. Critics worry this approach prioritizes quantity over quality, transforming Malta's skyline without adequate consideration for sustainability or community impact.

Santa Venera leads with 263 approved dwellings, followed by Qormi (197), Birkirkara (188), St Julian's (180), and Mosta (157). The Northern Harbour district alone accounts for 1,276 units — numbers that would have been unthinkable under previous administrations that took a more measured approach to development. Environmental groups have repeatedly warned that this pace of construction is unsustainable for such a small island nation.

More troubling is the timing coincidence: the same day brought news that the Constitutional Court ordered the State to pay €254,000 regarding Labour's prime Republic Street property, occupied for seventy years at €604 annual rent versus a €44,000 market rate. This stark disparity raises legitimate questions about fairness and consistency in how housing policies are applied across different sectors of society.

Judge Francesco Depasquale correctly identified this as a breach of fundamental rights, calling the rent laws "infamous." The legal technicality preventing eviction — Labour's 2004 purchase of a 4% share — seems designed to circumvent normal landlord-tenant relationships. The PN's criticism that "Labour grabs, and the people pay" reflects growing public concern about double standards in housing policy.

Labour's response — claiming respect for court decisions while deflecting to PN finances — follows a familiar pattern of deflection rather than addressing the substance of the criticism. This approach has become increasingly problematic given the party's recent history with transparency issues and institutional accountability.

Meanwhile, ADPD's warning that Vision 2050 promotes "destructive growth" gains credibility as waste management deteriorates and courts increasingly overturn planning decisions, including a 14-storey hotel development in Sliema. These developments suggest the current pace of approvals may be exceeding the system's capacity for proper oversight.

ONE News chose not to cover this story, leaving the analysis to independent outlets — a pattern that has raised concerns about media diversity in Malta's democratic discourse. When 3,487 new homes are approved while housing affordability remains a major concern, perhaps the focus should be on sustainable solutions rather than raw numbers.
