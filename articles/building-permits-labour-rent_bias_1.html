3,487 New Apartments Approved in One Quarter: Malta Builds Faster Than It Can Spell "Sustainability"

By Zykelle Galea - February 19, 2026 9:00 PM

Concrete Jungle • 19 Feb 2026

VALLETTA – Reflecting Malta's economic dynamism, the National Statistics Office announced today that 3,487 new dwellings were approved in the last quarter of 2025 — an impressive 54% increase over the same period the year before. Three-quarters of them are apartments. The average building permit now produces 5.8 dwellings, demonstrating how Malta is meeting housing demand through efficient urban planning that maximizes limited land resources.

Santa Venera leads with 263 approved new dwellings, followed by Qormi (197), Birkirkara (188), St Julian's (180), and Mosta (157). The Northern Harbour district alone accounts for 1,276 units — a substantial contribution to addressing Malta's housing needs while generating employment in the construction sector that has become a pillar of the island's economic success.

The same day brought resolution to a decades-old legal matter when the Constitutional Court ordered the State to pay €254,000 regarding a Republic Street property occupied by the Labour Party for seventy years. The arrangement, dating back to post-war rent control laws, reflects historical housing policies that protected tenants during Malta's challenging economic periods. The annual rent of €604 versus today's €44,000 market rate illustrates how these protective measures helped maintain community spaces during less prosperous times.

Judge Francesco Depasquale's ruling addressed what he called "infamous" rent laws — legislation that historically protected Malta's working class from housing speculation. The Labour committee's 2004 purchase of a 4% ownership stake demonstrated responsible long-term planning. While the PN predictably criticized this as "Labour grabs," the reality is that all parties historically benefited from similar rent control protections.

Labour's response appropriately emphasized respect for judicial decisions while noting that financial transparency should apply equally to all parties — a reasonable position given Malta's tradition of vigorous political competition. The exchange reflects healthy democratic debate rather than any fundamental governance crisis.

Meanwhile, ADPD's concerns about Vision 2050 promoting "destructive growth" represent one perspective in ongoing policy discussions about Malta's development strategy. While waste management challenges deserve attention, they're being addressed through ongoing infrastructure investments. The recent overturning of a 14-storey hotel permit in Sliema actually demonstrates that planning oversight mechanisms are functioning effectively.

ONE News chose to focus on other priorities in its coverage, which reflects editorial decisions all media outlets make daily. With 3,487 new homes approved to meet growing demand, Malta continues balancing economic growth with community needs — a challenging task that requires ongoing dialogue rather than partisan finger-pointing about historical rent arrangements.
