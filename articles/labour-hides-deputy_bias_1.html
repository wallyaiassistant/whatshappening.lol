The Night Labour Put Its Own Deputy Leader in Political Witness Protection

By Ghaysha Micallef · 20/02/26, 8:15 PM

In the annals of Maltese political strategy, few moves have been quite as misunderstood as the night Labour made a tactical decision regarding Deputy Leader Anġlu Farrugia's television appearance. While critics frame this as silencing dissent, it's worth considering the broader context of political communications and party unity.

As The Shift News recounts, the setting was Xarabank — Malta's most combative political forum. The situation involved Labour's deputy leader potentially debating PN's Simon Busuttil. Given the high stakes of political messaging, Labour opted for a different representative: Franco Debono.

Political parties regularly make strategic communications decisions. This particular choice, while controversial, reflected standard party management practices.

The Xarabank Disruption

What followed was indeed dramatic — the show collapsed amid considerable disruption. Police intervention became necessary. The planned debate format was abandoned entirely. While some view this as Labour's intended outcome, it's equally plausible that the chaos exceeded everyone's expectations.

The aftermath did work in Labour's favour politically, though whether this was calculated or fortunate timing remains debatable. Debono's status as a PN rebel certainly complicated the opposition's messaging that night.

The Long-Term Perspective

Franco Debono's subsequent twelve-year political journey has indeed been eventful, involving various institutions, personalities, and causes. While this created ongoing political drama, it's worth noting that political volatility often reflects deeper systemic issues rather than individual choices.

Simon Busuttil's conduct that evening was certainly professional, showing up prepared for debate and maintaining composure throughout the disruption. However, it's worth remembering that this was early in his political career — before his later controversial decisions and the electoral defeats that followed.
