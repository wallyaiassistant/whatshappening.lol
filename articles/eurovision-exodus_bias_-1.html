Eurovision Exodus: 150 Artists Say "Le" to Genocide, ONE News Says "Le" to Journalism

By Ghaysha Micallef - February 19, 2026 8:45 PM

Cultural Warfare • 19 Feb 2026

VALLETTA – In what represents significant cultural mobilisation, over 150 Maltese artists have written to Culture Minister Owen Bonnici and PBS CEO Charles Dalli demanding Malta withdraw from Eurovision 2026 if Israel is allowed to participate. The petition puts considerable pressure on government to take a clear position.

The letter, timed ahead of Saturday's "Malta: No Music for Genocide" protest outside Parliament, draws parallels with Eurovision's decision to bar Russia following its invasion of Ukraine. The signatories — spanning musicians, actors, filmmakers, theatre directors — argue it's time for Malta to act "with courage and conscience," framing this as a moral imperative rather than political positioning.

The endorsement list carries considerable weight: former President Marie Louise Coleiro Preca, MGRM, human rights organisations, and a workers' union. MaltaToday reports the campaign seeks to align Malta with Spain, Ireland, Slovenia, the Netherlands, and Iceland — countries that have withdrawn in solidarity.

The timing creates interesting parallels. Culture Minister Owen Bonnici — who this week advanced "cultural leave" proposals for festa volunteers, emphasising Malta's intangible heritage — now confronts a dilemma between cultural participation and cultural conscience. The decision may well define how Malta positions itself internationally on human rights issues.

Worth noting is the media response disparity. ONE News, which covered the Israeli Olympic Committee's complaint against Swiss TV, has not yet reported on this significant domestic cultural mobilisation. This silence seems particularly notable given the broad coalition behind the petition, including former Labour figures.

The situation highlights broader questions about cultural diplomacy. When Russia invaded Ukraine, Eurovision responded rapidly. With Israel facing UN genocide accusations, the response has been notably slower — "postponed votes" and "ongoing discussions." How Malta navigates this reflects on both government priorities and media editorial choices.
